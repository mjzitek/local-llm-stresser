A Field Guide to Local LLM Inference

Part I: The Hardware Floor

For most of the past three years, the dominant assumption in applied AI has been that serious work happens in someone else's datacenter. The argument was simple: frontier models are too large to fit on consumer hardware, the gap between hosted and local quality is enormous, and operating your own GPU is more headache than it's worth. Each of those three claims has weakened in the last twelve months, and a small but growing fraction of teams now find that running a 7B–70B parameter model on a workstation is not only feasible but, for some workloads, preferable.

The first thing to change was the hardware floor. Apple's unified-memory M-series chips put 64–192GB of GPU-addressable RAM into a laptop-sized box, and consumer NVIDIA cards now ship with 16–24GB of VRAM at price points that were unimaginable in 2021. The M3 Max and M4 Max architectures, in particular, blur the line between laptop and workstation: a fanless or near-silent machine with 128GB of unified memory can hold a Q4-quantized 70B model entirely in GPU-addressable RAM, with bandwidth in the 400–500 GB/s range. That is not state-of-the-art for inference — an H100 has roughly 8× the bandwidth — but it is enough for interactive, single-user workloads that would have been impossible on consumer hardware just two years ago.

NVIDIA's consumer line has moved in parallel. The 4090 ships with 24GB of GDDR6X at over 1TB/s of memory bandwidth, fast enough to run a 13B model at 80–100 tokens/second of decode throughput. The 5090, when it arrives at consumer prices, will push these numbers further. AMD's Radeon Pro series has finally received serious software support through ROCm, though the ecosystem still lags CUDA by a noticeable margin in real-world usability. Intel's Arc cards are an interesting third option for budget-conscious deployments, especially with the new Battlemage architecture targeting AI workloads explicitly.

Part II: Quantization in Practice

The second thing to change was quantization. Aggressive 4-bit and 5-bit quantization schemes — GGUF's K-quants, AWQ, GPTQ — preserve most of the quality of a full-precision model while cutting memory needs by 3–4×. A 13B model that needed 26GB of VRAM in fp16 fits comfortably in 8GB at Q4_K_M.

The headline numbers on quantization understate the picture. A naive reading of "Q4 vs fp16" suggests a 4× memory saving in exchange for some unspecified quality loss. The reality is more nuanced. K-quants — the GGUF family that includes Q4_K_M, Q5_K_M, and Q6_K — apply different bit-widths to different parts of the model, with the most "important" weights kept at higher precision. The result is that Q5_K_M is often statistically indistinguishable from fp16 on standard benchmarks, while Q4_K_M loses 1–3 points on reasoning-heavy evals.

AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantization) takes a different approach. Rather than quantizing weights uniformly, it identifies which weights are most sensitive to quantization based on the activation magnitudes they produce on calibration data, and protects those weights at higher precision. The result is that AWQ tends to outperform GGUF Q4 on the same memory budget, particularly for models that have not been specifically tuned for K-quant compatibility. The downside is that AWQ requires a calibration step on representative data, which is an additional friction during deployment.

GPTQ is the older sibling of AWQ, predating it by about a year and using a similar core insight: not all weights are equally important, so quantize accordingly. GPTQ uses a Hessian-based approximation to identify sensitive weights, which produces excellent results but is slow to compute. In practice, GPTQ has been mostly superseded by AWQ in production deployments, though the GPTQ ecosystem remains larger because it had a head start.

The cost of quantization shows up in long-tail behavior more than in average quality. A heavily quantized model is more likely to hallucinate when pushed past its training distribution, more likely to drift in long conversations, and more likely to fail on niche domains. For high-volume, well-bounded workloads — code completion, document classification, structured extraction — this rarely matters. For open-ended reasoning or creative work, the gap is real.

A practical recommendation: start with Q5_K_M for any new deployment. If memory is tight, drop to Q4_K_M and run an evaluation against your specific workload before committing. If memory is plentiful, Q6_K or Q8_0 are essentially free upgrades that close the gap to fp16. fp16 itself is rarely worth the memory overhead; the marginal quality improvement over Q8_0 is below the noise floor for most applications.

Part III: The Runtime Ecosystem

The third, and probably most underappreciated, change was the runtime ecosystem. llama.cpp, Ollama, and LM Studio have collectively turned what used to be a weekend of CUDA wrangling into a single command. All three expose the OpenAI chat-completions API, which means existing application code rarely needs to change to swap a local model in for a hosted one.

llama.cpp is the foundational project. Started by Georgi Gerganov in early 2023 as a hobby experiment in running LLaMA on a MacBook, it has grown into the de facto reference implementation for local inference. Its core innovation is GGUF, a self-contained binary format that bundles model weights, tokenizer, metadata, and quantization information into a single file. The runtime itself is written in C++ with no Python dependencies at the inference layer, which makes it portable to environments where CUDA-laden Python stacks are difficult to deploy.

llama-server is the binary you actually run for production-style deployments. It hosts a single model, accepts requests on the OpenAI-compatible /v1/chat/completions endpoint as well as native /completion and /infill endpoints, supports continuous batching for concurrent requests, and includes a web UI for interactive testing. Performance-wise, llama.cpp is competitive with any other open-source runtime on consumer hardware, and significantly ahead on Apple Silicon thanks to dedicated Metal kernels.

Ollama is a higher-level wrapper around llama.cpp that prioritizes ease of use. Models are pulled by name (`ollama pull llama3.1:8b`) from a curated registry, the daemon handles model loading and unloading transparently, and the same OpenAI-compatible endpoint is exposed by default. The trade-off is that Ollama is harder to tune for absolute peak performance — many of the knobs that llama.cpp exposes are not surfaced through the Ollama interface — and the daemon's automatic model swapping can introduce latency at the start of requests against models that aren't currently loaded.

LM Studio occupies a different niche. It's a desktop application with a GUI, primarily aimed at non-developer users who want to experiment with local models. Under the hood it uses llama.cpp (and, in newer versions, MLX on Apple Silicon), and exposes the same OpenAI-compatible API. For developers, LM Studio's value is mostly as a reference deployment — easy to set up, easy to demo, but typically not what you'd run in production.

A handful of other runtimes round out the ecosystem. vLLM is the standout for high-throughput batched inference, particularly on NVIDIA hardware. Its PagedAttention implementation is the reference for efficient KV cache management, and it routinely outperforms llama.cpp on large-batch throughput. The downside is that vLLM is significantly more complex to deploy, requires Python and a CUDA-capable GPU, and doesn't yet have a polished Apple Silicon story. SGLang is a newer entrant that focuses on structured generation and constrained decoding, with throughput numbers competitive with vLLM. MLX is Apple's own inference library, optimized for the Metal Performance Shaders and Apple Neural Engine pipelines.

Part IV: Throughput, Latency, and the Concurrency Curve

The most interesting metric for production deployment isn't single-request latency. It's how many concurrent users a single box can serve before tail latencies become unacceptable. This is where local inference gets surprising. A well-tuned llama.cpp server with continuous batching can serve 8–16 concurrent requests at acceptable latency on a single 24GB GPU, depending on context length distribution. That's a meaningful chunk of an internal team's workload, sometimes all of it.

The dynamics of batched inference are non-obvious. A batch of one request takes a fixed amount of time per generated token, dominated by the cost of moving model weights from VRAM to compute. A batch of eight requests doesn't take eight times longer — the weight movement is amortized across all requests in the batch. Throughput grows nearly linearly until you hit either the compute ceiling or the memory bandwidth ceiling, then plateaus. The exact shape of this curve depends on the model, the hardware, and the workload mix, but the qualitative pattern is consistent: small batches scale well, large batches plateau.

Continuous batching is the technique that makes this work. Rather than waiting for a fixed-size batch to fill before processing it, the runtime accepts new requests at any time and inserts them into the active batch as soon as a slot opens up. This requires careful management of the KV cache (each request has its own KV cache, and they must coexist in GPU memory), and it requires the model to handle requests at different stages of generation in the same forward pass. The combination is non-trivial to implement, which is why mature runtimes like vLLM and llama.cpp with their --parallel flag are significantly faster than naive implementations on concurrent workloads.

Time-to-first-token is the harder metric. It's dominated by prefill — running the entire prompt through the model to populate the KV cache before generation can begin. For a 4KB prompt on a 7B model, prefill takes 100–300ms on a consumer GPU. For a 32KB prompt, it can take seconds. This is why long-context workloads are surprisingly latency-sensitive, and why prompt caching is so valuable when the same context appears across multiple queries.

The latency budget for an interactive feel is around 200–300ms. Below that, the user perceives the response as instantaneous. Above 500ms, the lag is noticeable. Above a second, the interaction starts to feel broken. For voice agents specifically, the budget is tighter still: VAD (voice activity detection) and ASR (automatic speech recognition) consume 100–200ms, the LLM has to start producing tokens within another 100–200ms to maintain conversational flow, and TTS adds 50–100ms before the user hears the first audio. The end-to-end budget for "AI sounds responsive" is around 500–800ms, of which the LLM gets roughly half.

Part V: Memory Management and the KV Cache

The KV cache is the single most important data structure in transformer inference. As the model generates tokens, it caches the key and value tensors for each attention layer, so that subsequent tokens don't have to re-attend to the entire context from scratch. The cache grows linearly with sequence length, and at large context lengths it can dwarf the model weights themselves.

For a 7B model with 32 attention layers and a hidden dimension of 4096, the KV cache requires roughly 16KB per token at fp16. A 32K-token context therefore consumes 512MB of VRAM just for the cache, on top of the model weights. For a 70B model, the cache is closer to 80KB per token, and 32K of context consumes 2.5GB. As model architectures push toward longer contexts (1M tokens for Gemini, 200K for Claude), the cache becomes the dominant memory consumer.

Several techniques exist to manage this. PagedAttention, introduced by vLLM, treats the KV cache as a paged virtual memory system: each request's cache is split into fixed-size blocks that can be allocated, freed, and reused independently. This eliminates the internal fragmentation that plagued earlier batched-inference systems, where the cache had to be reserved for the maximum possible sequence length even if most requests were shorter. PagedAttention is now the reference implementation for batched LLM serving, and most modern runtimes have adopted some variant of it.

Quantization of the KV cache itself is another lever. Q8_0 quantization of the cache halves its memory cost with minimal quality impact; Q4_0 quantization quarters it but introduces noticeable degradation on long contexts. llama.cpp supports cache quantization out of the box; for memory-constrained deployments, dropping to Q8 cache is usually the lowest-cost optimization available.

Speculative decoding is a third technique that doesn't reduce cache size but does reduce the number of forward passes through the model. The idea is to use a small "draft" model to propose several tokens at once, then verify them with a single forward pass through the main model. If the draft is good (typical acceptance rates are 60–80%), this can deliver 2–3× speedups on decode throughput at the cost of running two models simultaneously.

Part VI: The Operational Picture

The case for local inference is not universal. Frontier reasoning models still live in the cloud, and they will for the foreseeable future. But for high-volume, latency-sensitive, or privacy-constrained workloads — code completion in an IDE, document summarization on confidential corpora, voice agents that need sub-300ms time-to-first-token — the math has quietly flipped. Sending every keystroke to a hosted model costs more than the workstation that would handle it locally, and the round trip alone often exceeds the budget for an interactive feel.

The economics deserve attention. A consumer workstation with a 4090 costs roughly $3000 amortized over three years, or $80/month. The same workload running against a hosted API for code completion at 1000 requests/day across a small team adds up to $200–500/month in API costs. The local box pays for itself in a few months and delivers better latency in the bargain. The picture changes for occasional, low-volume workloads, where the hosted API's pay-per-use model is hard to beat.

Privacy is the other major motivator. Many enterprises have policies that prohibit sending source code, customer data, or internal documents to third-party APIs. Local inference is not a silver bullet for compliance — it shifts the security boundary rather than eliminating it — but for organizations that already operate their own infrastructure, adding a local LLM is often easier than negotiating data-handling agreements with hosted providers.

What's missing, in most teams that have started experimenting, is a clear-eyed picture of what their hardware can actually do. "It feels fast" is not a benchmark. The interesting questions — how many concurrent users can a single box serve, what does latency look like at p95 versus p50, how does throughput collapse as context length grows — require deliberate measurement. The teams that take that step early tend to be the ones that ship local inference into production. The ones that don't tend to discover the limits in the worst possible way: in front of a customer.

Part VII: The Tooling Gap

Stress testing local inference is, today, a do-it-yourself affair. Hosted providers benchmark themselves at scale and publish results; local-runtime users mostly compare GitHub issue threads and Reddit posts. A standard benchmark suite — one that exercises the full latency surface, captures system metrics, and produces comparable numbers across runtimes — would meaningfully accelerate the field.

The shape of such a tool is becoming clear. It needs an adapter layer over OpenAI-compatible APIs (since that's the lingua franca of every local runtime). It needs workload generators for the obvious shapes: short-prompt high-QPS, long-context low-QPS, batched-uniform, mixed-realistic. It needs to capture per-request TTFT, decode tokens-per-second, and total latency, plus system-level CPU, memory, GPU utilization, VRAM, and thermals. And it needs to record everything to a queryable store so that yesterday's run can be compared to today's.

The metrics that matter, and the ones that don't, are not always obvious. Average latency is mostly meaningless; p95 and p99 are what determines user experience. Aggregate tokens-per-second on a single request is a vanity metric; concurrent throughput at acceptable latency is what determines deployment cost. GPU utilization above 80% is a sign that you're compute-bound; GPU utilization below 50% means you're either memory-bound or I/O-bound and there's headroom available. VRAM usage near the ceiling is a recipe for OOM under load spikes; aim for 80% of available VRAM as a comfortable working number.

None of this is hard. None of it is novel. What's been missing is the discipline to actually do it, in a form that other teams can adopt without a week of engineering effort. The current generation of local inference servers is good enough that the bottleneck is no longer the runtime — it's the absence of a culture of measurement around them.

Part VIII: Closing Thoughts

The shape of the next two years is, to my eye, predictable in its broad strokes. Hardware will continue to improve along its current trajectory: more VRAM at lower price points, more memory bandwidth, more accelerator silicon shipping in laptops and workstations. Quantization will continue to mature; we are probably one or two generations of techniques away from Q4-equivalent quality at Q3 memory cost. Runtimes will continue to consolidate around the OpenAI API surface, and the differentiation between them will increasingly be about operational characteristics — multi-tenancy, observability, integration with existing infrastructure — rather than raw performance.

What is harder to predict is the shape of the workloads themselves. Agentic patterns, with their long-running multi-turn loops and heavy tool use, have memory and latency profiles quite different from one-shot chat. Voice agents push end-to-end latency to its limits and reward different optimizations than text. RAG-shaped workloads with large retrieved contexts stress prefill rather than decode. The best stress-testing harness needs to evolve with the workload mix, and the teams building such harnesses need to keep their fingers on the pulse of what their applications actually look like.

The good news is that none of this is a moonshot. The hardware exists, the runtimes exist, and the techniques are well-documented. What's needed is engineering discipline — measure, deploy, monitor, iterate — applied to a domain that has so far benefited from rapid progress and benefited little from rigor. The teams that bring that rigor will be the ones who turn local inference from an enthusiast pursuit into a production-grade capability.
